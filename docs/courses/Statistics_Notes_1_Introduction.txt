The primary objectives of this course are:
• To provide a solid introduction to the mathematical theory behind statistical methods
• To offer theoretical guarantees for the statistical methods used in various applications

2. Select appropriate statistical methods for your specific problem

Why Statistics?

Statistics has been essential across diverse fields throughout history. Understanding randomness and making decisions under uncertainty are fundamental challenges that appear
in numerous contexts.

2.1

Historical Applications

2.1.1

Hydrology (Netherlands, 10th century)

When building dams and dykes, engineers faced the following problem:
• Dams should be high enough to prevent most floods
• Dams should not be excessively expensive (i.e., not too high)
This requires understanding the distribution of flood heights and making probabilistic
guarantees.
2.1.2

Insurance

Modern insurance relies heavily on statistical modeling:
• Given a person’s driving record, car information, and desired coverage
• What constitutes a fair premium?

Clinical Trials

Medical research depends on rigorous statistical analysis:
• A drug is tested on 100 patients
• 56 patients were cured, 44 showed no improvement
• Question: Is the drug effective, or could these results occur by chance?

2.2

The Common Thread: Randomness

Central Concept
All these examples share a fundamental characteristic: RANDOMNESS
Key questions arising from randomness include:
• Notion of average (“fair premium”, expected flood height, . . . )
• Quantifying chance (“most of the floods”, probability of cure, . . . )
• Assessing significance and variability

3

Probability vs Statistics

3.1

Probability: Working Forward

Definition 3.1 (Probability). Probability studies randomness when the underlying physical process is completely known.
In some cases, the random mechanism is perfectly understood: dice, cards, roulette,
fair coins, etc.
Example
Example 1: Rolling 1 Die
Consider the following game:
• Alice gets 1€ if the number of dots ≥ 3
• Bob gets 2€ if the number of dots ≤ 2
• Question: Who do you want to be: Alice or Bob?
Analysis:
• Alice wins with probability 4/6 = 2/3, expected value = 1 × 2/3 = 0.67€
• Bob wins with probability 2/6 = 1/3, expected value = 2 × 1/3 = 0.67€
Both have the same expected value, so the game is fair!

Example
Example 2: Rolling 2 Dice
• Choose a number between 2 and 12
• Win 100€ if you correctly predict the sum of the two dice
• Which number should you choose?
Solution: The sum 7 has the highest probability (6/36), as it can be obtained in
the most ways: (1,6), (2,5), (3,4), (4,3), (5,2), (6,1).
Key Insight: In probability, we know the random process (e.g., 1/6 chance for each
face, dice are independent), and we deduce the probability of outcomes and expected
amounts.

3.2

Statistics: Working Backward

Statistics becomes necessary when dealing with more complicated processes where:
• We need to estimate parameters from data
• True randomness exists (random sampling, biased coins, measurement error)
• Phenomena are deterministic but too complex to model exactly (statistical modeling)
Fundamental Modeling Principle
Complicated process = Simple process + Random noise
Good modeling consists of choosing a plausible simple process and an appropriate
noise distribution.

3.3

Contrasting Probability and Statistics

Example
Probability Perspective:
“Previous studies showed that the drug was 80% effective. Then we can anticipate
that for a study on 100 patients, on average 80 will be cured and at least 65 will
be cured with 99.99% probability.”
Statistics Perspective:
“We observe that 78/100 patients were cured. We conclude (with appropriate
methods) that we are 95% confident that for other studies the drug will be effective
on between 69.88% and 86.11% of patients.”
Key Distinction:
• Probability: Known mechanism → Predict outcomes

• Statistics: Observed outcomes → Infer mechanism

Statistical Model Assumptions

1. Each Ri is a random variable
2. Each Ri ∼ Ber(p) (Bernoulli distribution with parameter p)

4.6

Discussion of Assumptions

4.6.1

Assumption 1: Randomness

Randomness is a way of modeling lack of information. With perfect information about:
• The conditions of kissing
• What goes on in the kissers’ minds
• Physical positioning
Physics or sociology would theoretically allow us to predict the outcome deterministically. Since we lack this information, we model the outcome as random.
4.6.2

Assumption 2: Bernoulli Distribution

Since Ri ∈ {0, 1}, each Ri is necessarily a Bernoulli random variable.
More general model: We could assume Ri ∼ Ber(pi ) with different parameters for
each couple.
Problem: We don’t have enough information to estimate all the pi ’s accurately.
Simplification: We assume pi = p for all i (homogeneity assumption).
4.6.3

Assumption 3: Independence

The independence assumption is reasonable because:
• People were observed at different locations
• Observations occurred at different times
• There’s no obvious mechanism for dependence between couples

Requirements and Conditions for the CLT

Essential Requirements
1. Independence: The random variables X1 , . . . , Xn must be mutually independent
2. Identical Distribution: All variables must follow the same distribution
3. Finite Variance: The variance σ 2 = Var[X] must be finite (i.e., σ 2 < ∞)
Remark 5.1. These conditions can be relaxed in more advanced versions of the CLT:
• Lindeberg-Feller CLT: Allows for non-identical distributions under certain conditions
• Lyapunov CLT: Alternative conditions for non-identical distributions
• Dependent case: Extensions exist for certain types of dependence (e.g., mixing
conditions)
However, the classical CLT with i.i.d. assumptions is the most commonly used in practice.
When is n "Large Enough"?
The CLT is an asymptotic result (n → ∞), but in practice we work with finite
samples.
General guidelines:
• Rule of thumb: n ≥ 30 is often cited as sufficient for the CLT approximation
• Depends on the original distribution:
– Symmetric distributions: CLT works well even for smaller n (perhaps n ≥ 15)
– Highly skewed distributions: May need n ≥ 50 or even larger
– Distributions with heavy tails: Require larger n
– Nearly normal distributions: CLT approximation good even for small n
• Bernoulli case: CLT approximation is reasonable when np(1 − p) ≥ 10
Example
Assessing CLT Validity
For Bernoulli(p) variables:
• If p = 0.5 (symmetric): n = 20 might suffice
• If p = 0.1 or p = 0.9 (skewed): need larger n, perhaps n ≥ 50
• If p = 0.01 (highly skewed): need very large n, perhaps n ≥ 100
The condition np(1 − p) ≥ 10 helps ensure the normal approximation is reasonable.

5.2.8

Connection to Other Fundamental Results

Relationship with Law of Large Numbers
LLN vs CLT
Law of Large Numbers:
• Tells us: X̄n → µ (convergence)
• Answers: "Does the sample mean converge?"
• Type of convergence: Almost sure or in probability
Central Limit Theorem:
• Tells us: How fast and in what manner X̄n → µ
• Answers: "How is the sample mean distributed?" and "How fast does it
converge?"

Common Misconceptions

Important Note
Misconception 1: "The CLT says the data becomes normally distributed."
Correction: The CLT says the sample mean (or sum) becomes approximately
normal. The individual data points retain their original distribution.
Misconception 2: "I need n ≥ 30 regardless of the distribution."
Correction: The rule n ≥ 30 is a rough guideline. The required sample size
depends heavily on the original distribution’s shape.
Misconception 3: "The CLT applies to any statistic."
Correction: The classical CLT specifically applies to sample means. Other statistics may or may not be asymptotically normal (though many are, under appropriate
conditions).
Misconception 4: "Larger n always means the CLT works better."
Correction: While true asymptotically, if model assumptions (independence, identical distribution) are violated, increasing n won’t fix the problem.
5.2.12

Summary: Why the CLT is Fundamental

The Central Limit Theorem is arguably the most important result in statistics because:
1. Universality: Works for any distribution with finite variance
2. Normality: Explains why normal distributions appear so frequently in practice
3. Practical inference: Provides the foundation for confidence intervals and hypothesis tests
4. Rate quantification: Tells us how estimation error decreases with sample size
5. Asymptotic theory: Serves as the building block for more sophisticated statistical
methods
The CLT, together with the Law of Large Numbers, forms the mathematical foundation for understanding why statistical inference works and provides quantitative tools for
practical data analysis.

Practical Considerations

Model assumptions matter:
• Independence assumptions are often unverifiable but necessary
• Distributional assumptions (e.g., exponential, Bernoulli) should be justified
• Homogeneity assumptions (same parameter) are simplifications
Sample size considerations:
• CLT is asymptotic: requires “large enough” n
• Rule of thumb: n ≥ 30 for CLT to be reasonable
• For small samples, use exact methods or concentration inequalities
Interpretation of confidence intervals:
• Frequentist interpretation: The procedure has coverage probability 1 − α
• NOT: “The probability that θ is in this interval is 1 − α”
• The parameter θ is fixed; the interval is random
